How did it evolve over a bit more than a century?

1900s

It is hard to believe that before 1900, Broadway was a rural area. The inauguration of the Victoria theater and the Times Tower and, in 1904, the opening of the Times Square metro station, triggered a rapid urbanization. The area soon became the New Yorkers' favorite place for entertainment, musicals and vaudevilles.

1920s

Eye-catching lightbulbs became one of Broadway’s major symbols during the 1920s: visitors started to call it “The Great White Way”. The Theater District lived some of its most sumptuous years during the interwar period. Major successes such as Show Boat strengthened the prominence of musicals in Broadway. However, these years were quickly followed by the Great Depression, increasing political engagement in numerous shows.

1940s

The 1940s and 1950s are considered by many as Broadway Theater’s golden age. The production of musicals was extremely fruitful, as style was refined by Rodgers and Hammerstein’s first collaboration in 1943, with Oklahoma! . Some of Broadway's longest running plays were born during this period: Tobacco Road (1933), Live With Father (1939), South Pacific (1949)...

1960s

As America was facing drastic social changes in the 1960s, Broadway theater also had to rethink its established practices. Even though musicals such as Hello, Dolly (1964) encountered a huge success, Broadway’s creativity and productivity slowed down. The many revivals that were produced in the 1970s were symptomatic of this period.

1980s

With the rise of new technologies, Broadway goes global. A regenerated audience contributes to a new economic boom for the Theater District. In 1984, a production costs on average $3 million. Between 1980 and 1999 were launched some of Broadways’ most iconic classics: Les Misérables (1987), The Phantom of the Opera (1988), The Lion King (1997)...

2000s

Entering the new millenium, Broadway theater was stronger than ever. Times Square is now a must-do touristic attraction, and in 2016, and the district's 40 theaters attracted more than 13 million spectators. Lightbulbs have now mostly been replaced by LED billboards, but they are making Broadway's future even brighter.
